Deep Communication

Spring 2023 | Professor Allan Sayegh | Created in collaboration with T.D. Radhakrishnan

Deep Communication examined the potential of storytelling and message-sharing embodied beyond words alone. How does one truly capture the depth of self-experience and impart it to others? We explored both the remote and tangible, creating multisensory interfaces to share stories from the deeply personal to global scale. We captured biometric data—EEG readings, heart rate, and speech—and translated them into two interfaces: a real-time image generation system for remote storytelling, and a custom 3D-printed wearable reflecting the narrative itself.

Research Question

How can we develop a responsive communication system that effectively conveys incomprehensible self-experiences across body-scale and planetary-scale dimensions, transforming them into shared experiences that foster empathy and interconnectedness?

Remote Translation

The remote system incorporated several custom ML tools in a pipeline to intuitively feed an image generation system. As the user spoke, a language detector and transcriber fragmented their words into lines fed to the text-to-image model, allowing for automatic visual accompaniment without pausing verbal storytelling. We also incorporated a heart rate quotient, creating an added sensation via device vibration—allowing listeners to feel a rhythm behind the narrative.

The system was tested with a small user pool. Though none understood the originally spoken story (speaking different native languages), several indicated feeling slight empathy through the original speaker's tone alone, justifying the need for maintaining original speech. Accompanied by additional sensory narrative builders—visuals and heart rate—78% of users reported moderate to high degrees of self-indicated connectedness and empathy.

Fashion as Communication Investigation and Process

Fashion has long been a language of communication: graphic t-shirts signaling in-groups, the subtextual language of punk versus prep, expressions of power, spirituality, and ascendancy through clothing across cultures and centuries. Drag became a focus as a medium defined by storytelling through avant-garde garments—a rich history of tailoring and message-making.

I collaborated with Candace Persuasion, the Trans Health Representative at Mass General Hospital and a drag queen, to create a garment custom for her. At her shows, Candace frequently wore a bodysuit embroidered with "Trans Rights are Human Rights"—narrative through clothing was already her language. I wanted to weave new technology into that practice, enhancing the capacity to incorporate multidimensional aspects of storytelling into a custom garment.

I took a 3D photogrammetric scan of Candace to physically craft the garment, but to emotionally customize it, I had to capture her story. Under EEG and heart rate monitoring, she told the story of her transition—how drag helped her discover her femininity, and the difficulties surrounding coming to terms with her identity and living as her true self.

The EEG readings reflected turbulence: high theta waves indicated immersion in memory, spikes in alpha waves signaled heightened alertness or intensity, and beta waves suggested reflection and synthesis.

Final 3D-Printed Wearable

I went through many iterations of form and material studies, constructing the narrative piece for Candace.

I mapped these readings onto the body, designing a hyper-feminine form with modulating panels that oscillate to the recorded heartbeat from her story. Made from individually printed and assembled TPU panels, each portion was custom-modeled in Rhino.
