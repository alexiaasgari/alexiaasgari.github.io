Homunculus Architecture

Spring 2023 | Alexia Asgari, Lizzie Kripke, and Olivia Champ

The Homunculus is a concept taken from the field of neuroscience, where sensory sensitivity is mapped in the form of distorted scale of the human body. This project sought to revist this sommato-sensory model with updated scripting and artificial intellegence-driven modelling. We explored not only classic touch and motor sensitivity representations, but incorporated data from modern studies to translate thermal sensitivity on deformations of the human form. The models created culminated in a final wearable.

Research and Development

Sensitivity ranges around the body, and though we often describe it collectively, the idea of sensitivity draws from a variety receptors attuned to specific conditions around the body. We drew classic sensory and motor sensitivity from the original 1937 source of the neurological homunculus representation [PENFIELD, W. & BOLDREY, E. (1937) SOMATIC MOTOR AND SENSORY REPRESENTATION. BRAIN: A JOURNAL OF NEUROLOGY] and heating and cooling sensitivity from a more recent publication [LUO M. ET AL. (2020) HIGH-DENSITY THERMAL SENSITIVITY MAPS OF THE HUMAN BODY. BUILDING AND ENVIRONMENT V. 167]. These were presented numerically. We created visual maps of the body, isolating the individual components. We created a script that adjusted the values [level of lightness] on these particular components to create gradient maps of the body, that could then be plugged into our AI script in Houdini, that modified forms on a 3D body model in accordance with the value count at a degree determined by indivdually designed scaling formulas.

Generated Models

Depicted are a series of both 3D modeled exports from our Houdini workflow, and two 3D printed models. Each were named after the sensation they used to guide the transformation of the human form

Wearable

As a final representation of the distortion on the body, we mapped the "Hotmunculus" as a wearable for a physical body. We unrolled the 3D model to develop sewing patterns, and manually translated a physical representation of the generated form.
